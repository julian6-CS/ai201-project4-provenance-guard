Architecture overview: the path a submission takes from input to transparency label?

Once the user submits text to the provenance guard it will create a unique content id that can be used to later appeal any produced decision. An empty audit is then created, this is where the submission can be tracked and details like the content id or creator id can be stored for retrieval at a later time. The text from the submission is sent to the first signal, which measures and produces a score based on stylometric analysis to measure the probable influence of AI in the text. Afterwards, the text is passed along to the second signal which utilizes a llm, in this case, groq to measure how likely the text is produced by a llm to then produce a score. These two scores are combined with a skew favoring the llm analysis to create a attribution score. The score is then passed through a equation to produce the confidence score and the attribution label for the submission. This label, attribution score, confidence score, signal scores, and attribution is stored into the audit for quality measures. This audit is stored into a sqlite database where a user using the content id can later appeal this decision where the audit status is altered and the appeal reasoning is stored for human interaction.

Detection signals: what each signal measures, why you chose it, and what it misses?

Signal 1 (STYLOMETRIC HEURISTICS) : Specifically, this will measure the sentence length variance, vocabulary diversity, and the punctuation diversity within any input. Sentence length variance is a structural feature within writing that varies the most within human writing, in contrast, some AI-generated text has shown to be relatively uniform in this aspect. Evaluating vocabulary diversity and punctuation density is very useful since some AI-generated content follows a consistent punctuation pattern or limited vocabulary, but this metric only contributes a small portion to the signal. The reasoning behind this is that some users have a limited understanding of punctuation or deeply ingrained or learned habits that could be misclassified as AI. In addition to this, if a user writes on a genre or a topic foreign to their understanding, they are more likely to repeat certain words or phrases. The score provided by this signal is from 0-0.10 where sentence length variety makes up 52% of this scoring, meanwhile, vocabulary density and punctuation density each make up to 24% of this score. I chose this signal because there is some valuable evidence that can support the second signals decision to either reinforce or weaken the evidence provided by the semantic classifier in a inexpensive and explainable way. The score and their reasoning can be seen directly without any further input. As the name might imply, it largely misses the contextual evidence that is only possible by utilizing an llm and only focuses on the structural aspect of writing. The text can be produced by an AI but mimic the punctuation and structure shown typically by a human writer which is specifically why this best functions as a support for the llm decision.

Signal 2 (LLM CLASSIFIER): The input will be provided to an LLM where it will evaluate the text as a whole to analyze the semantic coherence, consistency of tone, discourse flow between sentences, predictable phrasing, use of formulaic language, whether the text is unusually balanced, linguistic patterns often seen in AI generated content, the use of generic or broad applicable statements, or use of phrasing associated with work produce by large language models. Since there is no equation or framework that we can provide to the llm where a statistic can be reliably calculated, the llm will make a holistic judgement and give a attribution score from 0 - 1.0 representing how AI-influenced the provided text is. We will also provide some guidance on how to base their numeric scoring. I chose this signal because it takes into account the semantic understanding of the text and it fills in a large blind spot with the first signal. There is also no other way to measure or analyze the relationships between the sentences and higher level writing patterns that cannot be captured without help from an llm. The large oversight when engaging with this signal is that it is nondeterministic and any user will be frustrated with a process that produces different outputs, from a user standpoint it misses the deterministic aspect from a formula or a process. Specifically, it also misses the many different features involved with the writing process like the authors identity or their writing history and the decisions from those submissions. External evidence that even a holistic analysis of a text would overlook but largely benefit from.

Rate limiting: the limits you chose and your reasoning for those specific values?

For rate limiting I chose 30 entries per hour and 240 entries per day, since the average texting speed of any person is one text every 90 minutes so 40 entries per hour is the maximum. This number of messages is very high and if an actual discusion is occuring then it would be much slower. Locking down entries to 30 entries will help mitigate those spamming messages and those trying to use tools to overload the system in place. This is then multiplied by eight since that is the average amount of time that a person can be near a computer or phone screen each day, resulting in the limit of 240 entries in one day.

Known limitations: at least one specific type of content your system would likely misclassify and why?

Much like the example provided, a song more likely within the rap genre often relies on repetition within the bridges, a consistent sentence length, and a reoccuring vocabulary meant for stylistic reasons. This can be observed in songs like "Gucci Gang" by Lil Pump or "Around the World" by Daft Punk where everything measured in Signal 1 remains consistent, heavily implying that it's AI-generated although it was created by a human. AI-generated can be altered heavily by a human or prompted to be human-like by prompting or editing in informal grammer, punctuation mistakes also possibly changes sentence structure, synonyms, and spelling mistakes that intentionally makes the text appear more human within Signal 2 and slightly in Signal 1. As a result, the system will assign it a lower AI confidence score although a majority of the text was generated through AI. I also see the Ai model misclassifying a users' human input as AI if it is well written with a variety of vocabulary and correct usage of grammer since there is an implicit expectation from the model and from people that LLM models are much more likely to produce work that is grammatically correct.

AI usage section: at least 2 specific instances describing what you directed the AI to do and what you revised or overrode?

I prompted Chatgpt to implement a method of creating the numerical score provided by entropy and utilizing word usage data, I provided it the specs and what I was trying to accomplish with signal 1 by providing it with further explanation of what I wanted to do. It provided the exact usage of entropy but I was unsatisfied since it normalized by using all the possible punctuation types or words while I wanted the score to reflect the evenness across all the observed distribution. This was also done to avoid any inputs containing a single punctuation or word category to avoid a bad input for normalization. In addition to that, I prompted Chatgpt a query to produce a SQLlite table for audit_log where it can hold everything I would possibly need for my implimentation, I provided it my full specs and some further clarifications but I was unsatisfied by its' coverage. I overrode some of the entries to add the appeal_reasoning, attribution score, and signal scores. I also made the content id a primary key to avoid any duplicate entries that could cause other functions I wanted to impliment into the workflow to fail or cause error.
